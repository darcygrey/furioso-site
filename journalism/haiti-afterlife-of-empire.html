Haiti, or the Afterlife of Empire

Colonialism did not die. It went offshore, learned the language of finance, and started charging interest.

FURIOSO 2025 Read · 8–12 min
SCROLL

There is a lie we like to tell ourselves in the West. It is a comforting lie, dressed in the language of progress, legality, and time.

The lie goes something like this: colonialism ended. Slavery was abolished. Empires retreated. Flags were lowered. Nations were born. The past, however regrettable, is finished business.

But history doesn’t end. It compounds.

If colonialism truly ended, Haiti would not look the way it does today.

The first crime: slavery as an industry

Haiti — once Saint-Domingue — was not just a colony. It was the colony.

By the late 18th century, it was the most profitable plantation economy on Earth. Sugar, coffee, indigo. Wealth poured into France at a pace that made European aristocrats salivate. That wealth was wrung from enslaved Africans worked to death and replaced as consumables.

This was not medieval bondage. This was industrialised slavery — racial, hereditary, mechanised, and legal. Human beings as capital assets. Death built into the business model.

France grew rich. Haiti bled.

The second crime: freedom with a receipt

Then something unprecedented happened.

The enslaved revolted — and won.

In 1804, Haiti became the first Black republic in the modern world and the only nation ever founded by a successful slave uprising. It shattered the moral architecture of empire. And empires do not forgive humiliation.

In 1825, France returned — not with chains, but with warships.

The terms were simple: Haiti could be recognised as a sovereign nation if it paid France 150 million francs to compensate former slave owners for their “lost property.” That property was human beings.

Unable to pay, Haiti borrowed — from French banks — at punishing interest.

Freedom was granted on credit.

This was not restitution. It was extortion dressed as diplomacy.

The third crime: debt as discipline

What followed was not a single payment, but a century-long financial chokehold.

Haiti refinanced, rolled interest into new loans, paid commissions and penalties, and exported its national income to Parisian banking houses. The debt was not fully discharged until 1947 — within living memory.

Schools went unbuilt. Roads remained dirt. Institutions never matured. Wealth that should have compounded inward flowed outward instead.

By conservative estimates, Haiti transferred the equivalent of tens of billions of dollars in today’s value — not through theft, but through contracts.

Colonialism had learned paperwork.

The West adapts

France was not alone.

In the early 20th century, the United States occupied Haiti, seized its customs revenues, and handed financial control to American banks. National City Bank of New York — now Citibank — took its turn at the tap.

This was not slavery. It was not even formal colonialism.

It was geopolitics.

And geopolitics, when asymmetric and sustained, produces colonial outcomes whether it admits to them or not.

Colonialism doesn’t vanish — it mutates

By the mid-20th century, the West congratulated itself. Slavery abolished. Empires dissolved. The moral arc bent, supposedly, toward justice.

But what replaced empire was not equality — it was dependency.

Debt. Trade rules. Extractive markets. Weak states inheriting borders designed to fracture them. Institutions hollowed out before they could stand.

Colonialism no longer needed governors. It needed spreadsheets.

Today, Haiti is described as a “failed state,” as though failure emerged spontaneously — as though history simply happened to it.

This is the final insult: erasing causality.

The uncomfortable question

France no longer collects payments. The United States no longer occupies Port-au-Prince. The contracts are closed.

But the effects remain.

Haiti did not simply fall behind — it was held back, systematically, for over a century. While Western nations compounded wealth, Haiti serviced debt imposed for the crime of self-liberation.

So the question is not legal. International law protects the past. Banks merge. States disclaim continuity.

The question is moral.

If wealth extracted under coercion helped build modern France, modern banks, modern power — does responsibility evaporate with time?

If colonialism has merely changed form, does justice require more than acknowledgements and aid packages?

And if the answer is no — if nothing is owed — then what exactly does the West believe responsibility means?

Haiti paid for its freedom.

The bill was never returned.

The interest, it seems, is still accruing.

History does not ask to be repaid. But it does demand to be recognised.

And recognition, sooner or later, always comes due.
