Sudan, or the Cartography of Control

Some countries are invaded with armies. Others are invaded with maps.

FURIOSO 2025 Read · 8–12 min

Sudan is one of those places the world keeps pointing at—usually with a grimace, sometimes with a shrug, occasionally with a slur—without ever admitting how much of the mess was built upstream, drafted in distant offices, and then continuously re-made by the people holding the steering wheel inside the country, too.

When Donald Trump reportedly used the phrase “shithole countries” in a 2018 immigration meeting—comments widely reported as aimed at Haiti and “African countries,” among others—he wasn’t offering analysis; he was offering a verdict.

But verdicts have a way of becoming lazy explanations. Sudan’s suffering gets filed under a single word—Africa—and then the file is closed. If you actually want to answer the question “why is Sudan suffering?”, you have to keep the file open long enough to watch the layers stack: colonial design, post-independence power grabs, resource politics, regional rivalries, and a state repeatedly pulled apart by men who preferred the spoils of control to the slow boredom of building institutions.

Start with the basic architecture. Modern Sudan was shaped under the Anglo-Egyptian Condominium declared in 1899: a joint sovereignty on paper, with British power doing the real governing, and Egypt bound into the arrangement in a way that would echo long after independence.

This wasn’t just a flag over a map, it was a system that organised the country for extraction and manageability, not cohesion. A classic move was to govern different regions differently, hardening internal divisions rather than healing them. British “Southern Policy” and related “closed districts” approaches in the early twentieth century—sold at times as protection, administration, or order—helped entrench a political and cultural split between north and south that would later turn combustible.

And across the same period, big development projects weren’t neutral gifts; they were engineered around imperial needs and export logic—cotton, revenue, control—rather than broad-based local prosperity. The Gezira irrigation scheme, inaugurated in the 1920s with colonial-era dam and canal infrastructure, is often described in the historical record as a cornerstone of cotton production for export.

Then comes the Nile—the river that behaves like a bloodstream and a border guard at the same time. Water in northeastern Africa is not just a natural resource; it’s a political instrument, and Sudan sits in the middle of a long argument where power has often flowed toward Cairo. The 1929 and 1959 Nile Waters agreements are frequently cited in scholarship and reporting as key moments that formalised Egypt’s dominant share and influence, shaping what upstream states could do and when.

Add to that the unresolved Egypt–Sudan border disputes—most famously around the Hala’ib Triangle, tied to competing boundary lines drawn in the late 19th and early 20th centuries—and you get a northern frontier that is never purely “a line,” but a constant political pressure point.

Sovereign Limits

None of this alone “kept Sudan underdeveloped,” because countries aren’t machines with one lever—but it did help shape a regional geometry where Sudan’s sovereignty has repeatedly had to negotiate with stronger neighbours over water, borders, and security.

But if colonialism wrote the rough draft, Sudan’s post-independence leadership has revised it with brutal enthusiasm. The country’s modern political story is crowded with coups, short civilian interludes, military dominance, and the chronic habit of ruling by emergency rather than consent—patterns that drain a state’s capacity even in peacetime.

By the time Omar al-Bashir’s regime fell in 2019 after mass protests, Sudan had already endured decades of civil wars, international isolation, and the violent centre-periphery politics that erupted catastrophically in places like Darfur. Those dynamics didn’t emerge from nowhere; they were fed by uneven development, identity politics, the militarisation of the state, and the deliberate outsourcing of violence to armed groups that could be denied when convenient.

That outsourcing matters because it leads directly to the nightmare Sudan is living through now. The war that began in April 2023 between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces has shredded infrastructure, public services, and any remaining sense that the centre can hold, producing mass displacement and a humanitarian catastrophe that agencies have described as among the world’s worst.

The RSF didn’t appear out of mist; it grew out of earlier militia structures tied to Darfur’s violence—an example of how a state that governs through armed proxies eventually discovers the proxies want the state. And once that happens, you don’t get “underdevelopment” as an abstract statistic; you get electricity grids destroyed, hospitals looted, water systems collapsing, universities emptied, and an economy that can’t breathe because the roads and ports and banks are no longer a national bloodstream—they’re trophies.

Zoom out and you see the other trap: even when Sudan has had moments where the international system seemed ready to help it stabilise—debt relief pathways, re-engagement, tentative investment—those openings have been fragile and political, and internal takeovers have repeatedly slammed the door.

In other words: foreign policy and finance can matter, but the domestic power struggle often decides whether the help becomes a foundation or just another interlude before collapse.

So who is to blame?

If you’re looking for a clean villain, you won’t get one without lying. Colonial rule shaped borders, governance habits, regional inequalities, and an export-oriented economy, while embedding Sudan in a Nile politics ecosystem where Egypt has long held formidable leverage.

That history matters because it loads the dice: it makes some futures easier than others. But Sudan’s leaders—and armed elites—also made choices, repeatedly, to prioritise control over competence, coercion over consent, and extraction over institution-building.

And regional and global actors have their own fingerprints: the arms, the money, the strategic courtships, the selective outrage, the aid that arrives late, the diplomacy that treats civilians as a footnote.

Trump’s insult, in that light, is less a diagnosis than a symptom: a powerful country looking at the wreckage—some of which its own system has helped produce in different ways around the world—and calling the wreckage proof of inferiority rather than evidence of history, politics, and human decisions colliding.

Sudan is suffering because it has been engineered and re-engineered under pressure: first by imperial management, then by postcolonial militarisation, then by resource and identity conflicts, and now by a war between rival centres of armed power.

It is suffering because a river that should be shared becomes a negotiating weapon, because borders drawn for administration become borders defended for pride, because development becomes a project for exports rather than citizens, and because every time a civil life tries to emerge—teachers, doctors, small businesses, students—the state keeps being dragged back into the gun’s grammar.

If you want a fair ending to this story, it starts with refusing the single-cause answer. Colonialism is part of the explanation. So is governance. So is war. So is geopolitics.

Sudan is not a “shithole”; it is a country caught in the long aftermath of decisions—some made abroad, many made at home—where ordinary people keep paying compound interest on the ambitions of men with uniforms and the patience of empires.
